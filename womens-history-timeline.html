Women's History

A Journey Through Courage, Resilience, and Revolution

Interactive Timeline
1776

The Foundation of a Nation

As the Declaration of Independence is signed, Abigail Adams writes to her husband John Adams, "Remember the ladies." She wasn't asking for voting rights, but for legal protection within marriages, as women were denied the right to be educated.

Abigail Adams
1830s

Second Great Awakening

A religious revival movement that transformed American society and gave rise to numerous reform movements, including early women's rights activism.

1848

Seneca Falls Convention

The birth of the women's rights movement. Elizabeth Cady Stanton introduces the Declaration of Sentiments, serving as a rallying cry for women's rights. This groundbreaking convention paved the way for women's suffrage.

Elizabeth Cady Stanton Lucretia Mott Susan B. Anthony
1860s

Civil War Heroes

Clara Barton, known as the "Angel of the Battlefield," tends to soldiers' wounds during the Civil War. Harriet Tubman, the "Black Moses," leads enslaved people to freedom through the Underground Railroad.

Clara Barton Harriet Tubman Sojourner Truth
1890

NAWSA Founded

The National American Woman Suffrage Association is created. Wyoming becomes the first state to grant women the right to vote, leading the charge for national suffrage.

Susan B. Anthony Frances Willard
1890s-1910s

Progressive Era Champions

Jane Addams founds Hull House for immigrants. Ida B. Wells exposes lynching in the South. Frances Willard leads the Women's Christian Temperance Union. These women reshape American society.

Jane Addams Ida B. Wells Frances Willard Emma Goldman
1917

First Woman in Congress

Jeannette Rankin becomes the first woman elected to Congress. A pacifist, she votes against entering both World Wars, standing firm in her convictions despite political pressure.

Jeannette Rankin
1920

19th Amendment Ratified

After decades of struggle, women finally win the right to vote. Alice Paul and Carrie Chapman Catt lead the final push, with Democrats credited for giving women suffrage.

Alice Paul Carrie Chapman Catt
1920s-1930s

Breaking Barriers

Amelia Earhart becomes the first woman to fly solo across the Atlantic. Margaret Sanger fights for reproductive rights despite the Comstock Act. Helen Keller advocates for people with disabilities.

Amelia Earhart Margaret Sanger Helen Keller Bessie Coleman
1930s-1940s

New Deal & WWII Era

Frances Perkins becomes the first female cabinet member, architecting Social Security and labor laws. Eleanor Roosevelt transforms the role of First Lady. Rosie the Riveter symbolizes women's wartime contributions.

Frances Perkins Eleanor Roosevelt Mary McLeod Bethune Dorothea Lange Billie Holiday
1942

Planned Parenthood Founded

Margaret Sanger establishes Planned Parenthood, revolutionizing reproductive health and rights for women across America.

Margaret Sanger
1948

Universal Human Rights

Eleanor Roosevelt helps draft the UN's Universal Declaration of Human Rights, establishing fundamental freedoms for all people worldwide.

Eleanor Roosevelt
1950s-1960s

Civil Rights Era

Pauli Murray refuses to give up her bus seat 15 years before Rosa Parks. Fannie Lou Hamer fights for voting rights in Mississippi. These women fuel the civil rights movement.

Pauli Murray Fannie Lou Hamer
1962

Breaking Political Barriers

Patsy Takemoto Mink becomes the first woman of color and first Asian-American woman in Congress, later co-authoring Title IX to prohibit gender discrimination.

Patsy Takemoto Mink
1963

The Feminine Mystique

Betty Friedan publishes "The Feminine Mystique," sparking second-wave feminism by challenging the notion that women could only find fulfillment as wives and mothers.

Betty Friedan
1965

Voting Rights Act

The Voting Rights Act passes, with Fannie Lou Hamer instrumental in its creation, ensuring voting rights for all Americans regardless of race.

Fannie Lou Hamer
1970s

ERA Battle

The Equal Rights Amendment divides the nation. Phyllis Schlafly leads conservative opposition, ultimately defeating the amendment while advocating for traditional gender roles.

Phyllis Schlafly
1973

Roe v. Wade

The Supreme Court legalizes abortion, marking a pivotal moment in reproductive rights and women's autonomy over their bodies.

1981

First Woman on Supreme Court

Sandra Day O'Connor becomes the first woman appointed to the U.S. Supreme Court by President Ronald Reagan, breaking the highest glass ceiling in American law.

Sandra Day O'Connor
1993

RBG Joins the Court

Ruth Bader Ginsburg becomes the second woman on the Supreme Court, championing gender equality through the Equal Protection Clause of the 14th Amendment.

Ruth Bader Ginsburg
2021

Breaking the Ultimate Barrier

Kamala Harris becomes the first woman, first African-American, and first Asian-American Vice President of the United States, shattering multiple glass ceilings at once.

Kamala Harris
